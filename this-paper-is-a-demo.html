Abstract

The content of this ‚Äòpaper‚Äô plays a secondary role, whereas the medium in which it is consumed through comes first. The characteristics of the medium in our demonstration has the most social and technical implications for the Web Science community, whether the audience is reading this on a screen or printed paper, listening to a screen reader, watching a movie, taking part in presentation, or even interacting with the document. Such varying experiences echo our quest to have the research community to re-examine the current practices for scholarly publishing and communication. Hence the question is, which mediums and behaviours for consumption, production, and dissemination of knowledge will benefit its ecosystem? Introduction

One of the most widely debated questions in the scientific community is the impact of digitization on the scholarly communication and knowledge exchange. Avenues in this discussion are targeting, for example, cloud research infrastructures, data sharing and citation techniques or new forms of publishing via nano publications. In this demo, we present a way how scholarly communication can truly digitized by means of living, interactive publications. Despite advances such as open and digital access to publications few has been changed yet with regard to the digitization of scientific publishing. Scientists still write static documents, which do not use the possibilities of digitization, such as interactivity, multimodality or semantic content annotation and representation.

In this demo, we present our Linked Scientific Publication approach for truly digitizing the concept of a scientific publication. The approach is based on using HTML5 and accompanying techniques such as CSS, JavaScript, SVG, RDFa for authoring and representing scholarly content. We demonstrate how multimodal content such as video and audio can be embedded. Especially for computer science code examples are an important type of content, which can be integrated in Linked Scientific Publications. We showcase, how different views of the content can be rendered for different devices (e.g. screen, print) or audiences (e.g. sideshows). A particular strength of the Linked Scientific Publication approach is the integration and linking of data, which can automatically update tables and diagrams when the underlying data changes. Also, all content can be annotated and represented using semantic knowledge representation formalisms to facilitate better search, exploration and retrieval of scholarly content. In the following subsections we explain and demonstrate each of these features of Linked Scientific Publications.

Technology and Design

Our work is called Linked Research. There is a single template using the HTML 5 Polyglot markup. Different CSS are used to present the information for different media e.g., academic paper layouts following the ACM and LNCS authoring style guidelines, slideshow styles, and can be observed even as a W3C "Recommendation" (to only demonstrate flexibility of semantic markup). JavaScript is applied to the document to progressively enhance the information's interactive components. In a nutshell, the minimum viable product encloses the following:

  • Documents are human and machine-friendly.
  • Using the plain old semantic HTML marking process, with further semantic annotations using microformats and RDF.
  • No server required. Works on local machine.
  • No installation.
  • No account creation.
  • No out-of-band tooling required. A Web browser is the only requirement, whether it is a Line Mode Browser, or Firefox Nightly. Content is foremost accessible and interactable just the same.
  • Licenses and rights: Apache License and CC0, and compatibles for any dependencies.

The code for Linked Research is publicly available [], and sample 'papers' (i.e., peer-reviewed and published papers) are available in the repository. The author guidelines for well-known LNCS and ACM themselves are also available as examples:

Presentation

The CSS are primarily tested using the Gecko browser engine e.g., Firefox, as it provides a more comprehensive and consistent CSS screen and print media support. The views are also tested for in other engines e.g., WebKit (Chrome, Chromium, Opera), and Trident (Internet Explorer). The flexibility of the single HTML viewed in different ways (i.e., potentially a CSS Zen Garden in its own right), can be tested from the document menu options. Users with supporting devices to trigger buttons can experiment with different views.

As browser engines improve their CSS implementations, so do these stylesheets, especially concerning the CSS Paged Media Module Level 3.

Interaction

Linked Research for screen devices where interaction is possible are enhanced as follows:

  • In browser editing without having to hand-code HTML or RDF syntaxes - partial support at the moment - and, sorting sections through table of contents.
  • Visibility of identifiers for sections and other important enough declared concepts. Fosters sharing and cross-linking of concepts, arguments, workflows etc.
  • Embed data in HTML: Turtle and JSON-LD
  • Document Metadata
  • Exporting to HTML. A (La)TeX export is planned.
  • Local Storage (in the browser) for offline editing. Auto-save is available.
  • Switching between different views e.g., ACM, LNCS, W3C-REC, Slideshow, Native.

Statistical Displays

Inline-charts or sparkline are intended to be concise and located where they are discussed in the text. They are treated as datawords carrying dense information. Edward Tufte describes sparkline as small intense, simple, word-sized graphic with typographic resolution [21]. For example, the GDP of Canada claimed by the World Bank Linked Dataspace is on the rise despite the market crash in 2008. This brief demonstration of Linked Statistical Data Sparkline, 1) compliments the supporting text without breaking the reader‚Äôs flow, and 2) provides an opportunity for the reader to investigate further by clicking on the data-line to access the source.

Linked Statistical Data Cube Designer

LSD (Linked Statistical Data) Cube Designer is a Web service with an user-interface for researchers to design their own statistical data cubes. The statistical objects that are part of the cube model are derived from and refer to Linked Statistical Dataspaces. It aims to lower the barrier for searching and reusing existing linked statistical components. LSD Cube Designer offers a way for researchers to search for existing cube component concepts from existing statistical linked dataspaces, select most suitable dimensions, measures, and attributes for their data structure and components, and then export the cube‚Äôs structural information (in RDF Turtle format). We can interact with this remote application in Figure [2]. For example, search for each component, e.g., dimension(s): "reference", measure(s): "value", and optionally attribute(s): "status", then see the button for more information, and select. Finally click on the Export Cube Structure button.
